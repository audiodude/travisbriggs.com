After fifteen-plus years of writing code, you'll find quite a few accumulated thoughts on how software gets built and why some approaches work better than others. [[javascript-typescript|JavaScript and TypeScript]] dominate most of the professional work around here - the ecosystem has grown from scrappy browser scripting language to the foundation of modern web development, for better and worse.

[[Python]] remains the go-to for quick scripts and data manipulation, though you'll discover dabbling in plenty of other languages over the years. [[Java]] was where teeth were cut on enterprise development, [[Perl]] taught the beauty and horror of text processing, and [[Ruby on Rails]] showed how convention over configuration could actually make sense. [[Vue.js]] represents the current take on frontend frameworks, though the JavaScript framework churn never really stops.

You might be interested to learn that the [[wp-1.0]] project has been one of the more significant contributions to the open source world - helping maintain the Wikipedia article assessment system that underlies offline content distribution. It's one of those invisible infrastructure pieces that millions of people depend on without knowing it exists.

Development tools matter more than you might think they would. You'll find notes on [[codecov]] for coverage analysis, [[github]] for collaboration and version control, [[apache]] for web server configuration when you need to get your hands dirty. The [[warnings-python]] system is the kind of unglamorous but crucial infrastructure that keeps large Python codebases from falling apart.

There's been watching the evolution of alternative protocols with interest. [[activity-pub]] represents a real attempt at decentralized social networking, while [[gemini]] offers a deliberately simpler alternative to the modern web. [[gemdown]] bridges some of these worlds, bringing markdown sensibilities to the gemini space.

The [[programming languages|broader question of language choice]] keeps evolving as the industry changes. What made sense five years ago might not make sense today, and what works for a team of three might be terrible for a team of thirty.
